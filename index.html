Introduction

There is currently increasing contention in the political landscape. Because of this, it is increasingly important to understand the factors that impact a candidates chance of success. In particular, this project will focus on the effect of funding on a candidates chances.

The purpose of this project is to examine relations between party, funding, and chances of election. Key questions will include: How much money is necessary to win an election? How does party affect funding? Can these factors accurately predict a candidate's chances of victory?

Data Curation

The Federal Election Committee collects data on the campaign contributions of every registered canditate for presidency, the Senate, and the House of Representatives. This data also includes the candidate's states, party affiliation, and whether they are incumbant, a challenger, or if the seat is open.

Data is sourced from https://www.fec.gov/data/browse-data/?tab=candidates

Primary Analysis

Since we have seen that there seems to be differences between funding recieved by Democrats and Republicans, but this does not correspond neatly to a linear model dependent solely on time, I will try to add some dimensions to predict the funding a candidate will recieve. Furthermore, I will try to create a model that functions well regardless of time, so it can be used to predict how much funding a candidate will recieve in the next election. Because the total amount spent varies by year, I will instead try to find the percentage of total funding that a candidate will get. Since this is a task with a low dimensionality and discrete variables, a decision tree will be a good choice. In addition, using a random forest will increase the accuracy of the analysis.

Insights and Conclusions

From the table, it can be seen that the state in which a candidate runs in the most significant factor in how much funding they will recieve by a large margin. The district that a candidate runs in is, on the contrary, the least important feature. This implies that the district feature might just be noise, and further analysis should not be more specific than the state level. Candidate office is similarly lowly weighted, implying Senators and Representatives recieve similar contributions. Party and Incumbant status have slightly higher impacts, meaning these measures should be kept. Because this model did not account for a lot of the variance, future analysis should consider using a different model and features not used here, such as google searches for a candidates names or positions on key issues.
